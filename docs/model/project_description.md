# Mapping GWAS Variants to Causal Effector Genes — and Their Direction

**What I built and investigated.** Genome-wide association studies flag thousands of trait-linked loci, but the causal *gene* usually stays hidden — the variant sits in non-coding regulatory DNA, often far from the gene it controls. I set out to test how well the field's default tool (cis-eQTL colocalization) actually resolves this, and to build a sequence-based alternative. I reimplemented a nascent-transcription enhancer→gene dataset (validated to machine precision against published pairs), embedded ~1.2M regulatory windows through a frozen Nucleotide-Transformer-v2 language model, and trained a two-tower convolution-attention encoder (CATE) that predicts both the enhancer→gene *link* and its *sign* — activation vs. repression.

**What I found.** On a benchmark of 1,160 GWAS loci with curated effector genes, colocalization names the correct gene at only **24.5%** of loci; it points to the *wrong* gene at 30.9% and is silent at 44.7% — three-quarters unresolved. My model does not beat a nearest-gene distance baseline outright, but it is **complementary**: it recovers 39 effector genes distance misses (18 at loci where colocalization is entirely silent), and — uniquely — it assigns a direction of effect (repressive-sign AUROC ≈ 0.62) that neither colocalization nor distance provides.

**Why it matters.** Effector-gene assignment is the bottleneck between a GWAS hit and a drug target: get the gene wrong and every downstream decision — which protein to drug, which pathway to follow — is wrong too. Quantifying exactly where the standard tool fails, and adding a sequence-only signal plus a first-of-its-kind effect-direction call, gives a more honest and more complete locus-to-gene map — and flags the loci where the community's default answer is silently incorrect.
